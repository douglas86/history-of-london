History of London Transportation system in the 1800s

A horse-bus or omnibus was a large horse drawn carriage that was mainly used in the 19th century in both United States and Europe. For the transportation of passengers. The driver would typically site on a front-facing bench normally on an elevated position outside from the passengers enclosed cabin.

There was also a smaller version available, which was normally used at hotels and restaurants to conveyor servants and luggage to and from there destination.

1662 – The first introduction of the Omnibus, launching in Paris.

1677 – 15 years later it was discontinues as fare prices started to increase and service was only restricted to high society members.

1824 - a man by the name of John Greenwood opened up the first bus line in Manchester, which no pre-bookings were required.

1827 - Stanislas Baudry, commissioned an English coach maker George Shillibeer to design a carriage that was capable of carrying a large volume of customers.

1828 – 28 April George Shillibeer’s design worked and was implemented as the first omnibus in Paris and began its journey between La Madeleine and la Bastille running every 15 minutes.

1831 – The first omnibus was introduced in London.

1833 – legislation was passed to allow these vehicles to plow the streets.
