History of a London Transportation system in the 2000s

London Underground

S Stock has now replaced the old A, C and D Stocks on the Metropolitan, Circle, Hammersmith & City & District lines

2000—Transport for London (TFL) formed under the Greater London Authority Act 1999.

2000—Transport for London (TFL) formed under the Greater London Authority Act 1999.

2005 - TFL criticized Metronet for awarding construction work to its shareholders.

2005—House of Commons Public Accounts Committee published a report questioning the value of the Public-Private Partnership (PPP) compared to a publicly run investment program.

2005—July 7 bombings caused significant disruption to underground services.

2006—Jubilee line extended with the addition of a seventh car to all trains.

2007—East London line closed for conversion into a London Overground line, later managed by TFL.

2007—Metronet went into administration, and TFL took over responsibilities.

2008 - TFL acquired the rights to the Oyster card.

2009—Circle line changed from a closed loop system to a spiral serving Hammersmith.

2010 - TFL took over Tube Lines, previously behind schedule on the Jubilee line upgrade.

2011—Introduction of new trains on the Victoria line.

2012—Over 4.3 million people used the Tube daily during the London 2012 Olympic and Paralympic Games.

2013—New signaling system allowed 33 trains per hour.

2013—Celebration of the Tube's 150th anniversary.

2015—Estimated passenger count reached 4.82 million passengers on some days.

2018 - Sub-surface lines (Metropolitan, District, Circle, Hammersmith & City) upgraded with new air-conditioned stock and track, electrical supply, and signaling system improvements.

2020—COVID-19 pandemic heavily affected the Tube, with reduced services and closed stations.

2021—Northern Line extension to Battersea upgraded and opened in September.

2022—Elizabeth line opened, increasing rail capacity and reducing cross-London journey times. The project also improved several central Underground stations.

Buses

2000—Passenger ridership experienced rapid growth, with a 19% increase.

2002—London's mainstay fleet of double-decker buses was supplemented by a fleet of 393 articulated buses to replace high-floor AEC Routemaster buses and meet increased capacity demands.

2003—Ridership reached levels not seen since the 1960s.

2006—London became one of the first major cities with an accessible, low-floor bus fleet, resulting in a 40% growth in ridership.

2008 - Boris Johnson committed to introducing a new Routemaster and removing bendy buses, leading to the New Bus for London competition.

2009—Articulated buses, or bendy buses, began to be withdrawn, starting with the Red Arrow fleet.

2010 - The design of the New Routemaster, an iconic standard bus, was unveiled by Mayor Boris Johnson.

2011 - The last Red Arrow bus was withdrawn from service.

2012—The New Routemaster designed by Heatherwick Studio and built by Wrightbus featured 2 staircases, 3 doors, and an open platform for passenger convenience.

2016 - Mayor Sadiq Khan limited the New Routemaster orders after procuring 1000 of the initially planned 2000 fleet.

1990—Initiatives to reduce emissions of the bus fleet, including the trial of hybrid and hydrogen fuel cell buses.

2006—Introduction of the first hybrid buses into service.

2014 - Battery-operated buses entered service.

2020 - TFL announced converting New Routemaster buses for front-door entry to reduce fare evasion.

2021 - Double-decker hydrogen fuel buses were introduced.

2021 - TFL announced a plan for all bus fleets to meet or exceed Euro V1 emission standards, phasing out older buses, and introducing new hybrid and electric buses.

2022 - A privately operated bus company began operating on Route A, a tourist-focused heritage route between Piccadilly Circus and London Waterloo.

2023—London's bus fleet consisted of 8,643 buses, including 3,835 hybrids, 950 battery electrics, and 20 hydrogen fuel cell buses.

2024—Certain bus routes, like SL8, X26, X68, and X140, joined the London Superloop express bus network, offering daily long-distance service with limited stops.

2037—The Mayor of London aims to achieve a zero-emission bus fleet.
