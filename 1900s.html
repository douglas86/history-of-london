History of a London Transportation system in the 1900s

London Underground

The Central line opened as the "Twopenny tube" in 1900. A Northern line train leaves a tunnel mouth just north of Hendon Central station.

1900 – the opening of a 3.562m diameter tunnel was dug beneath the roads between Shepherd’s Bush and Bank for the Central London Railway. Charging a flat fare of 2d (approximately 96p today) known as the “2-penny tube.” By the end of that year, it had carried nearly 15 million passengers.

Note: Initially electric locomotives hauled carriages, but the heavy locomotives caused vibrations that could be felt on the surface.

1900 – Central London Railway opened

1901 – a metropolitan and district joint committee recommended the Ganz three-phase AC system with overhead wires. This was accepted by both parties until the District found an investor, the American Charles Yerkes, to finance the upgrade. Yerkes raised £1 million (adjusted for inflation, £115 million) and soon had control of the District Railway.

1902 – an Underground electric railway company of London (UERL) was established to fund the district lines and complete operations of 3 tube lines.

1902 – 550 trains operated daily. Services began running to Upminster

1903 – the carriages were reformed into multiple units using a control system developed by Frank Sprague in Chicago.

1904 – Great Northern and city railway opened up

1904 – The great northern & city railway was built to take main line trains from the Great Northern Railway at Finsbury Park to the city at a terminus at Moorgate; however, the GNR refused permission for trains to use its Finsbury Park Station. So platforms were built beneath the station and public service on the line, using electric multiple units.

Note: With most of the District and metropolitan Railways using steam locomotives led to smoke-filled stations and carriages that were unpopular with passengers and electrification was seen as the way forward.

1905 – Metropolitan electricifed the new line from Harrow to Uxbridge and the route to the inner circle at Baker Street, the distict line electrified its unopened line from Mill Hill Park (now Action Town) to South Harrow and used these new lines to test its new trains and drivers.

1906 – Baker Street, Waterloo, Charing Cross, Euston, Hampstead, Great Northern, Piccadilly and Brompton Railway opened up and started its operations

1907 – District and Metropolitan Railway had electrified the underground sections of their lines.

1907 – The carriages were fitted with small windows and consequently were nicknamed padded cells. The C&CLR had extended from both ends, south to Clapham common and north to Euston.

1908 – Center London Railway (CLR) was extended to Wood Lane (near White city)

1912 – which was then extended to Liverpool Street

1915 – The underground was used as shelter during the Great War (also known World War 1) during Zeppelin air raids in June of that year.

1930 – Central and Northern lines formed part of the New Works Program.

1933 – London Passenger Transport Board (LPTB) was established. Which was there to oversee all the underground railways and all London area tram and bus operators.

1939 – During World War 2 all operations either halted or interrupted some work, and many tube stations were used as air-raid shelter.

1948 – London Passenger Transport Board (LPTB) was nationalized. With the mainline railways given priority over the maintenance of the Underground.

1953 – unpainted aluminum train entered service on the District line, which became the standard for all new trains.

1960 – the Metropolitan line was electrified as far as Amersham, with steam powered locomotives no longer hauled passenger trains.

1968 – the Victorian line opened in central London. With trains driven automatically.

1976 – Northern city line was overtaken by British Rail, and they linked it up with the mainline railway at Finsbury Park.

1979 – a new route, the Jubilee line, took over parts of the Bakerloo line, extending as far as Docklands. 1980 – the Travelcard and Capitalcard was introduced

1981 – Under the Greater London council, London Transport introduced a fare zone for buses and underground trains, cutting the average fare price.

1999 – before control was passed onto Transport For London (TFL), the London Underground was split up so that a public-private partnership (PPP) arrangement could be put in place.

1999 – extension of the rail from Docklands to as far as Stratford.

Note: In the early years of the 21st century, London underground was reorganized in a public-private partnership where private companies upgraded and maintained the infrastructure.

Buses

1902 – London General Omnibus Company (LGOC) started using motorized omnibuses

1904 – Thomas Tilling started his own first motor bus service

1906 – Bus numbers were first used.

1909 – from this year they were manufacturing their own motorized omnibuses.

1909 – Thomas Tilling and the LGOC entered into an agreement to pool all their resources. This agreement had restricted Thomas Tilling from expanding in London. Allowing LGOC to amalgamate most of London’s bus services. Thomas Clarkson had started a National Steam Car Company to run steam buses in London to be in competition with the LGOC.

1911 – The last recorded year of the horse-drawn bus

1912 – Underground Electric Railways Company of London (UERL) which at the time owned most of the London Underground, bought the LGOC.

1912 – The Associated Equipment Company (AEC) was created as a subsidiary of the LGOC. AEC built many of the local London buses.

1913 – Night buses began running

1919 – National Company had come to an agreement with the LGOC to withdraw from bus operations, with steam bus services ceasing later that year.

1922 – independent firms started. Using General route numbers, along with alphabetical suffixes to denote branch routes.

1924 – under the London Traffic Act, the Metropolitan Police was authorized to allocate route numbers, which all buses had to carry. Which led to chaos taken away from the police and handed once again to professional busmen.

Note: Decades later the suffixes were gradually abolished. The last such route in London was the 77A, which became the 87 in June 2006.

1933 – LGOC along the rest of the UERL became part of the new London Passenger Transport Board (LPTB)

1933 – 1947 The LPTB oversaw transport.

1933 – 2000 London buses continued to operate under the London Transport name.

Note: LPTB, under Lord Ashfield assumed responsibility for all bus services in the London Passenger Transport Area. The area had a radius of about 30 miles from Central London. Later becoming London Transport’s green buses.

1940 – a bus that was operating on route 68 was abandoned by its passengers and crew on Belham high road seconds before a bomb fell, creating a huge crater which the bus rolled.

1948 – 1962 it was nationalized and became the London Transport Executive.

1950 – 4.5 billion passengers were taking the bus at the time.

1957 – RTL 780 riding on route 7 collided with a queue of people at a bus stop in Oxford street, resulting in 8 deaths. The driver had collapsed of heat exhaustion.

1962 – there was a dramatic decline in passenger transport to 3.1 billion passengers.

1963 – 1969 The responsible authority for London Transport was then successively the London Transport board.

1966 – The introduction of the Red Arrow bus express routes all numbered in the 500s.

1968 – The Red Arrow bus network was expanded.

1968 – The last bus specifically designed for London was the AEC Routemaster.

1969 – a new law transferred the green country services, outside the area of the Greater London council, to the National bus Company.

1970 – Trading under London country, green buses and green line coaches became the responsibility of a new NBC subsidiary, London country bus services.

1970 – 1984 Becoming the Greater London council

1974 – Jill Viner became the first female bus driver for London Transport.

1980 – Margaret Thatcher’s government decided to privatize the bus operating industry in Great Britain.

1980 – several newly emerging minibus and midibus models in a bid to up the frequency on routes, these buses dropped off to the level of niche operation on routes not suitable for full size buses.

1984 – 2000 and changing again to the London Regional Transport

1985 – bus routes were gradually tendered out to private companies

1989 – London buses were split into business units

1990 – The Red Arrow bus was gradually phased out.

1990 – business units were sold off in the private sector

1993 – London was on the earliest major users of the first low-floor single decker vehicles entering service

1998 – the first low-floor double-decker vehicles entering service
