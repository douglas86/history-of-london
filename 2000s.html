History of a London Transportation system in the 2000s

London Underground

S Stock has now replaced the old A, C and D Stocks on the Metropolitan, Circle, Hammersmith & City & District lines

2000 – The (Transport For London) TFL was formed as part of the Greater London Authority by the Greater London Authority Act 1999.

2003 – The control was passed to Transport for London (TFL)

2003 – Bakerloo, Central, Victoria, Waterloo & City lines (BCV) as well as the sub-surface lines, metropolitan, district, circle, east london, hammersmith & city lines (SSL). The Contract was won by Metronet.

2005 – April, TFL had criticised Metronet, saying it had given capital construction work to its shareholders

2005 – March, House of Commons Public Accounts Committee, charged with ensuring value for money in public spending, published a report concluding that it was “impossible to determine” whether the PPP was better value than a publicly run investment program.

2005 – 7 July, 3 bombs exploded on the underground trains, two on the Circle line at Aldgate and edgeware road and the third on the Piccadilly line between king’s cross and russell square. Taking underground services more than a month to restore services.

2006 – Adding of a seventh car to all trains on the Jubilee line

2007 – East London line closed, to be converted into a London Overground line.

2007 – Metronet went into adminstration for the TFL, with TFL taking over responsibilities

2007 – East London line was closed to be converted to a London Overground line, this line was operating as an isolated shuttle since 1939

2008 – with the liquidation of metronet, the UK government tried to find another private firm to fill the vacuum. TFL was the only viable option.

2008 – TFL bought the rights to the oyster card

2009 – December the Circle line changed from serving a closed loop around the center of London to a spiral also serving Hammersmith.

2009 – Circle line had stopped serving a closed loop system around the Centre of London on the north side of the River thames to a spiral serving Hammersmith.

Note: between July 2009 and June 2011 new trains were introduced on the Victorian Line.

2010 – Transport for London (TFL) had been opposed to take full financial failure of the infrastructural from privately owned companies.

2010 – TFL took over Tube Lines

2010 – Tube lines were behind schedule on the Jubilee line upgrade and after conflict arose over the costs of future work in that year’s PPP review. TFL bought Tube Lines for £310 million.

2010 – Building of the Crossrail project at a cost of £18.8 billion which connected two mainline railways with a new east-west tunnel under central London.

2011 – The commissioning of an automatic operational signaling system

2012 – over 4.3 million people were using the Tube on some days while the London 2012 Olympic and Paralympic Games.

2013 – With the new signaling system in place, it would allow 33 trains per hour from January of this year.

2013 – The Celebration of the tube turning 150 years old. With celebratory events such as steam trains and installation of unique Labyrinth artwork at each station.

2015 – December this year the Tube had an estimated passenger count of 4.82 million passengers on some days.

2018 – The sub-surface (Metropolitan, District, Circle, Hammersmith & City) lines have been upgraded, with new air-conditioned Stock. Also in the same year, the sub-surface track, electrical supply and signaling system are also being upgraded in a programmed allowing an increased peak-hour capacity. Replacement of a single control room for the sub-system was established in Hammersmith and an automatic train control (ATC) system which has been there since the 1940s.

2020 – With the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic heavily affecting the tube, with overground government was urging people to only catch the tube if necessary so that it could be used by critical workers. During March, all services on the Waterloo & City line were suspended with 40 tube stations were closed on the same day. During the pandemic, some escalators were switched off the elevating the number of passengers only allowing a certain number of passengers on their platform. Some statistics showed that the tube was experiencing some of the lowest passenger journey numbers since the 1800s.

2021 – There was an upgraded program on the Northern Line extension to Battersea.

2021 – September the Northern Line extension opened. Which extended the Northern line from Kennington to Battersea Power Station via Nine Elms.

2022 – The Elizabeth line opened to increase rail capacity and reduce cross-London Journey times. Although not part of the Underground, the line connects with many Underground stations, with the project rebuilding and expanding several central Underground stations including Tottenham Court Road.

Buses

2000 – Ridership rose rapidly with 19% growth in passengers

2002 – the mainstay fleet, double-decker buses, were augmented with a fleet of articulated buses, rising to a peak fleet size of 393 Mercedes-Benz Citaros. Which were introduced to replace the high-floor AEC Routemaster and to cope with an increased capacity.

2003 – Ridership at a level last seen since the 1960s

2006 – London became one of the first major cities in the world. To have an accessible, low-floor bus fleet.

2006 – Ridership growth was at 40% in London

Note: The bus fleet grew to cope with demand, from around 5,500 buses to over 8,000. Traffic speeds across London improved, and levels of air pollution in central London decreased.

2008 – Boris Johnson made several commitments to change the London Buses vehicle policy, namely to introduce a new Routemaster and remove the bendy buses. Transport for London announced the New Bus for London competition.

2008 – London Travelwatch had conducted research that replacing articulated buses with double-decker buses would be more expensive. More vehicles would be required to maintain those buses.

2009 – Commissionioner of Transport for London Peter Hendy announce that the withdrawal of the bendy buses. This was to reduce the cost to TFL; the articulated buses would be withdrawn after a 5-year operating contract came up for renewal, with a replacement bus being considered.

2009 – The first bus to be withdrawn was the Red Arrow fleet on routes 507 and 521 in May of that year.

2010 – Mayor of London, Boris Johnson unveiled the design of the New Routemaster, the proposed replacement for the Routemaster as an iconic standard bus which was used for exclusive use in and around London.

2011 – The last Red Arrow bus was withdrawn on routes 507 and 521

2011 – Autocar praised the New Routemaster on a road test, rating it ahead of contemporary and historically designed buses.

2012 – buses that were designed by Heatherwick Studio and built by Wrightbus featured 2 staircases, 3 doors and an open platform allowing passengers to hop on and off.

2016 – Mayor of London, Sadiq Khan decided that now more orders were to be placed as only after 1000 of the 2000 that Johnson had envisaged fleet had been procured.

1990 – efforts underway to reduce emissions of the bus fleet. Early work to replace the older buses like the AEC Routemaster and fitting particulate filters to exhaust.

2000 – hybrid and hydogen fuel cell buses were trailed

2006 – The first hybrid buses entered service.

2012 – it was recommended that every bus after this date should be a hybrid bus. That was later dropped.

2014 – Battery-operated buses first entered service.

2020 – Transport for London (TFL) announced a New Routemaster would be converted so that passengers only enter by the front door. As to reduce fare evasion.

Note: London has the 2nd largest bus fleet in Europe with over 970 battery electrics and hydogen fuel cell buses in service.

2021 – Double decker hydogen fuel buses were introduced

2021 – the announcement of all bus fleets to meet or exceed Euro V1 emission standards, with them trying to phase out of older buses and retrofitting of diesel vehicles and the introduction of the new hybrid & electric buses.

2022 – a privately operated bus company started operating on Route A, which was a tourist focused heritage route between Piccadilly circus and London Waterloo.

2023 – London has 675 bus routes served by over 8600 buses, of which almost all are privately operated companies under contract to London buses, part of the publicly owned Transport for London.

Note: Over 970 buses in the fleet are battery electric and hydrogen fuel cell buses. Being the second-largest zero emission bus fleets in Europe behind Moscow.

2023 – the last two routes of the Red Arrow buses were withdrawn

2023 – The London bus fleet total was at 8,643 buses included. Which includes 3,835 hybrid buses, 950 battery electric buses, and 20 hydrogen fuel cell buses.

2024 – A small number of bus routes (namely routes SL8, X26, X68 and X140) run a daily service, traveling long distance and serving few stops in its route. This is expected to become part of London Superloop express bus network.

2037 – Mayor of London is aiming for a zero-emission bus fleet
