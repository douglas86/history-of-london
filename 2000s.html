History of London Transportation system in the 2000s

London Underground

S Stock has now replaced the old A, C and D Stocks on the Metropolitan, Circle, Hammersmith & City & District lines

2000 – The (Transport For London) TFL was formed as part of the Greater London Authority by the Greater London Authority Act 1999.

2003 – The control was passed to Transport for London (TFL)

2003 – Bakerloo, Central, Victoria, Waterloo & City lines (BCV) as well as the sub-surface lines, metropolitan, district, circle, east london, hammersmith & city lines (SSL). Contract were won by Metronet.

2005 – April, TFL had criticised Metronet, saying it had given capital construction work to its shareholders

2005 – March, House of Commons Public Accounts Committee, charged with ensuring value for money in public spending, published a report concluding that it was “impossible to determine” whether the PPP was better value than a publicly run investment programme.

2005 – 7 July,3 bombs exploded on the underground trains, two on the Circle line at Aldgate and edgeware road and the third on the Piccadilly line between king’s cross and russell square. Taking underground services more than a month to restore services.

2006 – Adding of a seventh car to all trains on the Jubilee line

2007 – East London line closed, to be converted into a London Overground line.

2007 – Metronet went into adminstration for the TFL, with TFL taking over responsibilities

2007 – East London line was closed to be converted to a London Overground line, this line was operating as an isolated shuttle since 1939

2008 – with the liquidation of metronet the UK government tried to find another private firm to fill the vacuum. TFL was the only viable option.

2008 – TFL bought the rights to the oyster card

2009 – December the Circle line changed from serving a closed loop around the center of London to a spiral also serving Hammersmith.

2009 – Circle line had stopped serving a closed loop system around the Centre of London in the north side of the River thames to a spiral serving Hammersmith.

Note: between July 2009 and June 2011 new trains were introduced on the Victorian Line.

2010 – Transport for London (TFL) had been opposed to take full financial failure of the infrastructural from privately owned companies.

2010 – TFL took over Tube Lines

2010 – Tube lines were behind schedule on the Jubilee line upgrade and after conflict arose over costs of future work in that year’s PPP review. TFL bought Tube Lines for £310 million.

2010 – Building of the Crossrail project at a cost of £18.8 billion which connected two mainline railways with a new east west tunnel under central London.

2011 – The commissioning of an automatic operational signaling system

2012 – over 4.3 million people were using the Tube on some days while the London 2012 Olympic and Paralympic Games.

2013 – With the new signaling system in place it would allow 33 trains per hour from January of this year.

2013 – The Celebration of the tube turning 150 years. With celebratory events such as steam trains and installation of unique Labyrinth artwork at each station.

2015 – December this year the Tube had an estimated passenger count of 4.82 million passengers on some days.

2018 – The sub-surface (Metropolitan, District, Circle, Hammersmith & City) lines have been upgraded, with new air-conditioned Stock. Also in the same year the sub-surface track, electrical supply and signalling system are also being upgraded in a programmed allowing an increased peak-hour capacity. Replacement of a single control room for the sub-system was established in Hammersmith and an automatic train control (ATC) system which has been there since the 1940s.

2020 – With the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic heavily affecting the tube, with overground government was urging people to only catch the tube if necessary so that it could be used by critical workers. During March all service on the Waterloo & City line were suspended with 40 tube stations were closed on the same day. During the pandemic some escalators were switched off the elevate the amount of passenger only allowing a certain number of passengers on there platform. Some statistics showed that the tube was experiencing some of the lowest passenger journey numbers since the 1800s.

2021 – There was an upgraded program on the Northern Line extension to Battersea.

2021 – September the Northern Line extension opened. Which extended the Northern line from Kennington to Battersea Power Station via Nine Elms.

2022 – The Elizabeth line opened to increase rail capacity and reduce cross London Journey times. Although not part of the Underground, the line connects with many Underground stations, with the project rebuilding and expanding several central Underground stations including Tottenham Court Road.
