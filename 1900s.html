History of a London Transportation system in the 1900s

London Underground

1900-The Central London Railway opened with a 3.562m tunnel between Shepherd’s Bush and Bank, charging a 2d fare (approximately 96p today). Carried nearly 15 million passengers, faced vibration issues from heavy locomotives.

1901—Metropolitan and District railways recommended a Ganz three-phase AC system. District found investor, Charles Yerkes, gaining control.

1902—Underground Electric Railway of London (UERL) established to fund district lines, operating 550 daily trains, extending to Upminster.

1903 - Carriages reformed into multiple units using Frank Sprague's control system.

1904—Great Northern and City Railway opened, using electric multiple units beneath Finsbury Park.

1905 - Metropolitan electrified Harrow-Uxbridge line and District Line's Mill Hill Park-South Harrow for testing new trains.

1906—Baker Street, Waterloo, Charing Cross, Euston, Hampstead, Great Northern, Piccadilly, and Brompton Railway began operations.

1907—District and Metropolitan electrified underground sections, carriages nicknamed "padded cells," C&CLR extended.

1908—Central London Railway (CLR) extended to Wood Lane and later Liverpool Street in 1912.

1915—Underground used as shelter during World War I Zeppelin air raids.

1930—Central and Northern lines part of New Works Program.

1933—London Passenger Transport Board (LPTB) established to oversee underground and London area transport.

1939—World War II interrupted operations, tube stations used as air-raid shelters.

1948 - LPTB nationalized, mainline railways prioritized over Underground maintenance.

1953—Unpainted aluminum trains introduced on District line.

1960—Metropolitan line electrified to Amersham, steam locomotives discontinued.

1968—Victoria line opened with automatic trains.

1976—Northern City Line linked with mainline railway at Finsbury Park.

1979—Jubilee line introduced, extending to Docklands.

1980—Travelcard and Capitalcard introduced, simplifying fares.

1999—London Underground reorganized under public-private partnership.

1999—Rail extension from Docklands to Stratford completed.

Buses

1902—The London General Omnibus Company (LGOC) introduced motorized omnibuses.

1904 - Thomas Tilling initiated his own motor bus service.

1906—Bus numbers were implemented for the first time.

1909—LGOC began manufacturing their own motorized omnibuses.

1909 - Thomas Tilling and LGOC agreed to merge resources, giving LGOC control over most of London's bus services. Thomas Clarkson's National Steam Car Company posed as competition.

1911—The last recorded year of horse-drawn buses.

1912—Underground Electric Railways Company of London (UERL), the majority owner of London Underground, acquired LGOC.

1912—The Associated Equipment Company (AEC) became a subsidiary of LGOC, constructing many London buses.

1913—Night buses were introduced.

1919—The National Company withdrew from bus operations, ceasing steam bus services.

1922—Independent firms adopted General route numbers with alphabetical suffixes indicating branch routes. Metropolitan Police authorized route numbers, later managed by busmen.

1933—LGOC and UERL joined the new London Passenger Transport Board (LPTB) overseeing transport until 1947. London buses operated under the name London Transport until 2000.

1940 - A bus operating on route 68 was abandoned seconds before a bomb fell, creating a crater the bus rolled into.

1948 - 1962 - Nationalization under the London Transport Executive, with 4.5 billion passengers using buses in 1950.

1957—RTL 780 on route 7 collided with a queue of people at a bus stop by oxford street station, causing 8 deaths due to the driver's heat exhaustion collapse.

1962—Passenger transport declined dramatically to 3.1 billion passengers.

1963 - 1969 - London Transport was overseen successively by the London Transport Board.

1966—Introduction of Red Arrow bus express routes in the 500s, later expanded.

1968—Last AEC Routemaster specifically designed for London.

1969—Green country services outside Greater London council's area transferred to the National Bus Company.

1970—London Country emerged as an NBC subsidiary.

1970 - 1984 - a Greater London council was responsible for London Transport.

1974 - Jill Viner became London Transport's first female bus driver.

1980 - Margaret Thatcher's government privatized the bus operating industry.

1984 - 2000 - London Regional Transport managed London buses.

1985—Bus routes were tendered out to private companies.

1989—London buses were organized into business units.

1990—Red Arrow buses were phased out.

1993—London was an early user of low-floor single decker vehicles.

1998 - Low-floor double-decker vehicles were introduced.
