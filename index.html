History of a London Transportation system in the 1800s

Omnibus

A horse-bus or omnibus was a large horse-drawn carriage that was mainly used in the 19th century in both the United States and Europe. For the transportation of passengers. The driver would typically site on a front-facing bench normally on an elevated position outside from the passengers enclosed cabin.

There was also a smaller version available, which was normally used at hotels and restaurants to conveyor servants and luggage to and from there destination.

1662 – The first introduction of the Omnibus, launching in Paris.

1677 – 15 years later it was discontinued as fare prices started to increase and service was only restricted to high-society members.

1824 - a man by the name of John Greenwood opened up the first bus line in Manchester, which no pre-bookings were required.

1827 - Stanislas Baudry, commissioned an English coach maker George Shillibeer to design a carriage that was capable of carrying a large volume of customers.

1828 – 28 April George Shillibeer’s design worked and was implemented as the first omnibus in Paris and began its journey between La Madeleine and la Bastille running every 15 minutes.

1831 – The first omnibus was introduced in London.

1833 – legislation was passed to allow these vehicles to plow the streets.

1850 – Thomas Tilling started horse bus services

1855 – London General Omnibus Company or LGOC was funded to regulate the horse-drawn omnibus services operating in London

Note: as horses could only work for a limited number of hours in a day, as they had to be housed, groomed, fed, and they produced large amounts of manure which omnibus companies had to store and dispose of. Many systems needed to have 10 or more horses at a stable for each bus system.

1860 – with the rise of mass steel being produced horses were then put onto rails were they could move 3 to 10 times as many people.

1890 – with the rise of electric propulsion being used, it started to replace the horse-drawn cart in all major cites.

1914 – 4 Aug, the last horse-drawn cart between Peckham and Honor Oak Tavern

London Underground

Fowler's Ghost was an experimental fireless steam locomotive designed by John Fowler to prevent smoke and steam underground. It was not considered a success, and condensing steam locomotives were used.

1830 – The first proposal of an underground railway networking linking the city of London

Note: In the first half of the 19th century, London had grown greatly and the development of a commuting population arriving by train each day led to traffic congestion with carts, cabs and omnibuses filling the roads.

1850 – there were several railway terminals located around the urban center of London

1852 – Charles Pearson a Solicitor to the City of London was a leading promoter of several schemes and the creation of the City Terminus Company to build such a railway from Farringdon to King’s Cross.

Note: with the City of London supporting the idea, the railway companies were not interested and the company struggled to proceed.

1854 – The Metropolitan Railway (known as the Met) was granted permission to build an underground line at an estimated cost of £1 million.

1860 – Construction only started until March, as the met found it hard to raise the capital with the Crimean War under way.

1863 – the world’s first underground railway opened its doors. The Metropolitan Railway, using gas-lit wooden carriages hauled by steam locomotives working with the district Railway.

1863 – 10 January, a 6 km railway was opened to the public. Mostly built using the “cut-and-cover” method from Paddington to King’s Cross, east of King’s Cross was built by tunneling and then followed the culverted River Fleet in an open cutting to the new meat market at Smithfield. Carrying in at about 38000 passengers on the opening day, within the first 12 months it had carried 9.5 million passengers. Within the 2nd twelve months this increased to 12 million.

Note: With the Met’s early success it prompted a group of application to parliament the new railways in London, many competing for similar routes. The House of Lords established a select committee that recommended an “inner circuit of railway that should join nearly all the principal railway terminals to the Metropolis”.

1864 – The Metropolitan Railway had been extended soon after opening, reaching Hammersmith with the Great Western Railway.

1869 – passage was dug through the London Clay under the Thames from Great Tower Hill to Pickle Herring Stairs near Vine Street (now Vine Lane)

Note: A circular 2.1m tunnel was dug 410m using a wrought iron shield. A method that had been patented in 1864 by Peter William Barlow. A railway was laid in that tunnel

1870 – August, a wooden carriage conveyed passengers from one side to the other. Later that year the company went bankrupt as the tunnel was considered to be uneconomical, they then converted it to pedestrian use, known today as the Tower Subway.

1871 – the District began operating its own trains, the railway had extended to West Brompton and a terminus at Mansion House. Hammersmith was reached from Earl’s Court and serves reached Richmond, Ealing, Hounslow and Wimbledon.

1877 – The Met & St John’s Wood Railway opened as a single track branch from Baker Street to Swiss Cottage. This was to be the Met’s most important route as it expanded north into the Middlesex countryside, were it stimulated the development of new suburbs.

1879 – with the Met now wishing to access the South Eastern Railway via the East London Railway (ELR), Act of Parliament was obtained to complete the circle and link to the ELR.

1880 – Harrow was reached and the line was extended to Verney Junction in Buckinghamshire more than 80 km from Baker Street and the center of London.

Note: towards the end of the 19th century, the railway shared tracks with the Great Central Railway route out of Marylebone.

1884 – 17 September, an official opening ceremony of the completing and train run of the Circle Line

1884 – 6 October, starting of the circle line

1884 – The completion of the Circle line, which expanded the railway service by more than 50km from Baker Street and the center of London.

1884 – October that year the Circle line was completed.

1886 – Construction of the city and south London Railway (C&SLR) was started by James Henry Greathead.

Note: 3.1m circular tunnels were dug between King William Street (close to today’s Monument station) and Elephant and Castle. From Elephant and Castle, the tunnels were a slightly larger 3.2m to Stockwell. These series of tunnels were bored under the surface so that they didn’t need any agreements from property owners.

1890 – The first deep-level tube line, the city and South London Railway with electric trains.

1898 – The waterloo and city railway opened its doors

1898 – the District began to serve Whitechapel as a result of the expansion.
